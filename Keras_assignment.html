Final Thoughts¶

This model was faily simple to train, the extensive dataset is certainly a key factor for this. There's a moderately healthy ratio between the dataset size and number of inputs and classes. I started with a model similar to the ones I've seen to be successfull for MNIST since on principle both datasets are very similar, however, I slowly migrated to a model that is heavy on the input and lighter towards the output, I suppose this structure might promote recognition of a lot of patterns, which are then correlated in further layers to finally be narrowed down to the output classes, however, this is just a possible explanation of why this appears to work. I the end, I have a 784>2048>128>10 architecture trained to produce a 89.61% accuracy with the test dataset.

Final Thoughts¶

This dataset was notably harder to fine tune and never managed to get it over 80% without significant overfitting. Two of the most prominent reasons I consider significant to this is the size of the dataset (it being relatively small at a total size of 11227 data points) and the ratio of the size to the amount of inputs and outputs (46, way more compared with the IMDB dataset).

To couteract these problems I figured filtering the inputs to discard common and meaningless words, such as 'the', 'he', 'she', 'as', etc., could significantly reduce the useless noice in the input, I was able to improve results by filtering the top 150 words. This approach could be taken further by adding another layer of text processing that can determine what words are really important in terms of conveying content, topic and data, and thus, only feeding this network words that have a stonger correlation with the genre.

Given the complexity of the data and the grat number of classes I felt it necessary to try a big network, after trying several combinations 2048 seemed the best number for which the increase in complexity yielded sufficient improvement to be worth it.

Final Thoughts¶

This was definitively the trickiest dataset. I found it to be nearly impossible to get over 70% accuracy in the test dataset. With 397 usable entries, this dataset is just too small for a NN to trully learn the hidden patterns inside the data, even for a person there's no easy way of structuring so that the logic becomes discernible. I would attribute the training dificulty to one main factor: insifficient data, both in terms of dataset size and available features. As en educated guess, it appears that there must be another feature this dataset is not taking into account for determining admissions. Maybe experience counts, maybe extracurricular activities, maybe school of origin, something is probably missing from the picture.

In the end, I found out that given the small size of the dataset, aiming for smaller networks yielded the best results.
